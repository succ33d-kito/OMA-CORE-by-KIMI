# Theory of O.M.A.-C.O.R.E.

## A Model of Reality, Expressed as Hypotheses

This document states the theoretical assumptions underlying O.M.A.-C.O.R.E. Every statement is a hypothesis. None is claimed as absolute truth. Each is subject to revision as evidence accumulates.

---

### Hypothesis 1: The World Produces Continuous Information

The world continuously generates information through the interaction of physical, economic, social, and political systems. This information production is not directed at any observer. It exists independently of whether it is detected, understood, or acted upon.

**Corollary:** The total information produced at any moment exceeds the processing capacity of any finite system. Selection is inevitable.

---

### Hypothesis 2: Most Information Is Noise

The vast majority of information produced by the world is not meaningful — it is random fluctuation, measurement artifact, or locally irrelevant detail. It carries no signal about future states.

**Corollary:** A system that treats all information as equally meaningful will be overwhelmed by noise. Filtering is not optional. It is the primary function.

---

### Hypothesis 3: Small Numbers of Related Events Form Meaningful Clusters

Meaningful consequences rarely emerge from single events. They emerge when multiple independent events converge toward the same possible outcome. A single rate cut means little. Rate cuts, combined with weak dollar data, gold inflows, and mining sector strength, form a cluster pointing toward a precious metals consequence.

**Corollary:** Event counting is not enough. Event relationships matter. The same events in different configurations produce different consequences.

---

### Hypothesis 4: Consequences Emerge Gradually

Consequences do not appear instantly. They emerge over time as evidence accumulates, correlations strengthen, and clusters form. An emerging consequence goes through stages:

1. **Unrecognized** — too little evidence for any observer to detect
2. **Early recognition** — a small number of observers detect the pattern
3. **Developing consensus** — evidence accumulates, more observers agree
4. **Mature recognition** — the consequence is widely understood and priced in
5. **Resolution** — the consequence materializes or is invalidated

The value of recognition decreases as the consequence matures. Early recognition has the highest value. Late recognition has zero or negative value (the crowd has already acted).

**Hypothesis:** The best opportunities are consequences at stage 2 — early enough that the majority has not recognized them, but with enough evidence that the hypothesis is testable.

---

### Hypothesis 5: Every Hypothesis Is Provisional

A hypothesis is a structured belief about a possible consequence. It is never certain. It contains:

- Current evidence — what supports the hypothesis right now
- Supporting evidence — what would strengthen it
- Contradicting evidence — what would weaken it
- Confidence — how reliable the evidence appears
- Reliability — how trustworthy the evidence sources are
- Unknowns — what the system does not know
- Possible invalidation — what would disprove the hypothesis

A hypothesis that cannot be invalidated is not a hypothesis. It is a belief. O.M.A.-C.O.R.E. should hold hypotheses, not beliefs.

---

### Hypothesis 6: Criterion Develops Through Validated Experience

Criterion is not taught. It is not programmed. It is not downloaded from a model. It emerges from repeated cycles of:

1. Form a hypothesis
2. Act on the hypothesis (or explicitly decide not to)
3. Observe the outcome
4. Compare outcome to hypothesis
5. Classify the error (if any)
6. Update the hypothesis
7. Accumulate the lesson

Each cycle produces a small improvement in judgment. Over many cycles, these improvements compound into operational criterion.

---

### Hypothesis 7: Memory Enables Learning, Learning Enables Criterion

Without memory, every outcome is a isolated event. Patterns cannot be detected. Lessons cannot be accumulated. Each decision starts from zero.

With structured memory:

- Outcomes are stored with their hypotheses
- Patterns across outcomes become visible
- Errors can be classified and tracked
- Improvements can be measured over time
- Criterion can be calibrated

Memory without structure is just storage. Structured memory — where outcomes are linked to hypotheses, evidence, and decisions — enables learning.

---

### Hypothesis 8: Learning Requires Outcome-Based Feedback

Learning requires a feedback loop. The loop has four stages:

1. **Decision** — the system commits to an action or explicitly refrains
2. **Outcome** — the world produces a result
3. **Comparison** — the system compares outcome to hypothesis
4. **Update** — the system adjusts its judgment

The quality of learning depends on the quality of the comparison. A binary outcome (win/loss) produces shallow learning. A decomposed outcome (which parts of the hypothesis were correct, which parts were wrong) produces deep learning.

---

### Hypothesis 9: Criterion Improves Future Decisions

Criterion is the accumulated ability to judge what matters. It is not a model. It does not produce predictions. It produces calibrated judgments about:

- How much evidence is enough
- When a hypothesis is ready for action
- Which consequences are worth tracking
- Which correlations are real vs spurious
- When to hold, when to act, when to wait
- When a hypothesis has been invalidated

Improved criterion leads to better decisions. Better decisions produce better outcomes. Better outcomes produce more evidence. The cycle continues.

---

### Hypothesis 10: No Theoretical Claim Is Final

Every hypothesis in this document is subject to revision. If evidence accumulates that any hypothesis is wrong, it should be updated. The theory is a living document.

**Unknowns that remain:**

- At what scale does cluster formation become detectable above noise?
- How many events are needed before a cluster is meaningful?
- What is the optimal balance between hypothesis diversity and hypothesis focus?
- Can criterion be transferred between domains (trading to creation to entrepreneurship)?
- How long does it take for operational criterion to develop to a useful level?
- Is there a ceiling on criterion development, or does it continue improving indefinitely?

These are not weaknesses in the theory. They are questions that the system itself should help answer.

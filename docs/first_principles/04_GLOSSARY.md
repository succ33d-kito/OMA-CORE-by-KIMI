# Glossary of O.M.A.-C.O.R.E.

*Official vocabulary. Terms defined precisely to avoid ambiguity.*

---

### Data
Raw, unprocessed observations from the world. Prices, volumes, headlines, social signals. Data has no meaning until it is interpreted. It is the lowest level of abstraction.

**Distinction:** Data is *what* happened. It becomes information only when placed in context.

---

### Information
Data interpreted within a context. A price of 50,000 is data. A price of 50,000 after a 10% drop from yesterday's close is information. Context transforms data into information.

**Distinction:** Information adds context to data. It answers the question "what does this data point mean relative to something else?"

---

### Event
A discrete occurrence in the world that produces information. An event has a timestamp, a type, a source, and a set of attributes that distinguish it from background noise.

**Distinction:** Not all information arrives as events. Some information is structural (market hours, trading rules, instrument definitions). Events are changes in state.

---

### Knowledge
Information that has been tested against reality. A hypothesis that has been validated or invalidated through outcome observation becomes knowledge. Until then, it remains opinion.

**Distinction:** Knowledge is information with evidence. Opinion is information without evidence. The threshold between them is the outcome.

---

### Correlation
A measurable relationship between two or more events or variables. Correlation does not imply causation. It implies only that two things move together more often than chance would predict.

**Distinction:** Correlation is observed. Causation is inferred. O.M.A.-C.O.R.E. should track correlations but must not confuse them with causes.

---

### Cluster
A group of related events and correlations that point toward the same possible consequence. A cluster is not just many events. It is many events that, taken together, reinforce, contradict, or modify a consequence hypothesis.

**Distinction:** Many events is quantity. A cluster is structure — the relationships between events matter as much as the events themselves.

---

### Consequence
A possible future state of the world that may emerge from observed events, correlations, and clusters. A consequence is a prediction — but not a prediction about a specific price. It is a prediction about a possible future configuration of the world.

**Distinction:** A prediction says "price will go up." A consequence says "the conditions for a price increase are forming." The difference is specificity and time horizon.

---

### Hypothesis
A structured, recorded belief about a consequence. Contains the triggering events, supporting correlations, related cluster, expected consequence, confidence estimate, evidence inventory, invalidation conditions, and time horizon.

**Distinction:** A hypothesis is not a prediction. It is a framework for testing a prediction. A hypothesis survives or is invalidated by evidence. A prediction is simply right or wrong.

---

### Opportunity Candidate
A consequence that could potentially be acted upon but has not yet passed the threshold for action. The system judges it worth tracking but not yet worth committing capital.

**Distinction:** Opportunity candidate tracks "might be worth doing." Actionable opportunity is "worth doing now." The difference is the threshold.

---

### Actionable Opportunity
An opportunity candidate that has accumulated enough evidence and confidence to justify action. The system commits resources.

**Distinction:** Action is the commitment. The opportunity classifies as actionable when the expected value of acting exceeds the expected value of waiting.

---

### Decision
The act of choosing between alternatives: execute, wait, replace, reduce, increase, ignore, monitor. A decision always involves at least two alternatives, even if one is "do nothing."

**Distinction:** A decision is a commitment to a course of action (or inaction). It is distinct from a signal, which is a recommendation. Not all signals lead to decisions.

---

### Action
The execution of a decision. In trading: opening a position. In creation: publishing content. In entrepreneurship: making a business decision. Action is where the system interfaces with the world.

**Distinction:** Action is the observable manifestation of a decision. A decision without action is contemplation. An action without a decision is reflex.

---

### Outcome
The measurable result of an action. Profit or loss. Engagement or silence. Advantage gained or lost. An outcome is what actually happened, as opposed to what was expected to happen.

**Distinction:** Outcome is reality. It is the feedback signal that validates or invalidates the hypothesis that led to the action.

---

### Memory
Stored outcomes, decisions, hypotheses, evidence, and events. Memory is the raw material from which knowledge is extracted and criterion is developed.

**Distinction:** Memory is storage. Learning is the process of extracting patterns from memory. Without memory, learning cannot occur.

---

### Learning
The process of extracting patterns from memory and using them to improve future decisions. Learning transforms outcomes into knowledge and knowledge into criterion.

**Distinction:** Learning is the process. Knowledge is the intermediate state. Criterion is the accumulated capability.

---

### Evidence
Data and information that supports or contradicts a hypothesis. Evidence has direction (for or against), weight (how strongly it supports), and reliability (how trustworthy its source is).

**Distinction:** Evidence is not fact. Evidence is information relevant to a hypothesis. The same information may be evidence for one hypothesis and irrelevant to another.

---

### Confidence
A numerical estimate of how likely the system believes a hypothesis is to be correct. Confidence should be calibrated: when the system says 70% confident, it should be right 70% of the time.

**Distinction:** Confidence is an estimate. Accuracy is a measured fact. Calibration is the alignment between them.

---

### Reliability
A measure of how trustworthy a source of evidence is. A source that has been consistently accurate over time has high reliability. A source that has been frequently wrong has low reliability.

**Distinction:** Reliability is about the source. Confidence is about the hypothesis. A hypothesis based on reliable sources should have higher confidence.

---

### Criterion
The accumulated ability to judge what matters. Criterion is not an engine, a model, a database, or an algorithm. It is an emergent capability produced by the interaction of memory, learning, hypotheses, evidence, outcomes, knowledge, context, and time.

**Distinction:** Criterion is the output of the system's learning process. It is not a thing that can be built. It is a capability that emerges.

---

### Wisdom
Criterion applied consistently over long time horizons across multiple domains. Wisdom is what criterion becomes after years of operation across diverse conditions.

**Distinction:** Criterion is domain-specific (trading criterion, creation criterion). Wisdom is domain-general — the ability to judge what matters across different contexts.

---

### Noise
Information that carries no signal about future states. Noise is not inherently meaningless — it may be meaningful in a different context or at a different scale. It is noise relative to the system's current questions.

**Distinction:** Noise is context-dependent. What is noise for a trading system may be signal for a social system.

---

### Signal
Information that carries reliable, actionable content about future states. A signal is noise that has been validated — it has demonstrated predictive power in the past.

**Distinction:** Signal is noise that passed the test of time. All signal was once candidate signal. Not all candidate signal becomes signal.

---

### Truth
Correspondence between a hypothesis and reality. Truth is the asymptotic limit of evidence accumulation. It is approached but never fully reached.

**Distinction:** Truth is the direction of learning, not its destination. The system should aim to have hypotheses that are less wrong over time, not to achieve absolute truth.

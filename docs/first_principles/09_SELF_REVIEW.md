# Self-Review

*A critical evaluation of the first-principles documents for internal consistency, weak assumptions, and open questions.*

---

## 1. Internal Consistency

### Strengths

The documents are broadly consistent. Key concepts are carried across documents without contradiction:

- **Criterion** is defined the same way in the Manifesto, Theory, Discoveries, Glossary, and Criterion document — as an emergent capability, not a component.
- **Evidence supremacy** appears in the Laws (Law 10), Constitution (Article 1.1), and Design Principles (Principle 2) — consistent framing.
- **Provisional nature of knowledge** is stated in Theory (Hypothesis 10), Laws (Law 9), and Constitution (Article 6.1).
- **Consequence-to-opportunity chain** is consistent across the Theory, Glossary, and Discoveries — events produce correlations, correlations form clusters, clusters suggest consequences, consequences become hypotheses, hypotheses translate to opportunities.

### Potential Contradictions

**Structured memory vs. emergent criterion.** The Glossary defines memory as "stored outcomes, decisions, hypotheses, evidence, and events" and criterion as "an emergent capability." But the Criterion document states that criterion "emerges from the interaction of memory, learning, hypotheses, evidence, outcomes, knowledge, context, and time." If criterion is truly emergent, it cannot be directly designed or built. Yet the Discovery document discusses criterion as something to be "measured" and "developed." This is not a contradiction, but it creates a tension: the system can create the conditions for criterion to emerge, but cannot produce criterion directly.

**Evidence sufficiency.** The Constitution requires evidence for decisions, but the early system has limited evidence. The Theory acknowledges this (Hypothesis 10 lists unknowns). The tension is between making decisions with limited evidence (necessary in early stages) and the principle of evidence-first decision-making. The resolution is that evidence quality should match claim strength — early claims require less evidence, but also carry less confidence.

**Simplicity vs. capability.** The Design Principles state "simple before complex" and the Constitution says "default to no." Yet the long-term vision includes components like Consequence Engine, Cluster Intelligence, and Hypothesis Tracking — which are not simple. The resolution is that complexity must justify itself through demonstrated benefit, not be assumed valuable. The documents are consistent on this point — they just set a high bar for adding complexity.

## 2. Weak Assumptions

### Assumption 1: Consequences Can Be Detected Reliably

The entire theory rests on the assumption that consequences — possible future states of the world — can be detected with useful accuracy from available information. This is unproven. It is a hypothesis that the system itself should help validate.

**Risk:** If consequences cannot be detected above noise, the entire framework collapses. The system may need to operate at the correlation or cluster level without reaching consequence detection.

### Assumption 2: Criterion Compounds Over Time

The thesis assumes that criterion, once developed, persists and compounds. This may not hold. Criterion may decay if not continuously exercised. The system may forget or overwrite old lessons with new ones.

**Risk:** Criterion may require continuous maintenance. A system that stops learning may also stop having criterion.

### Assumption 3: Failure Types Are Classifiable

The Failure Intelligence discovery assumes that failures can be categorized into meaningful types. In practice, failures often have multiple causes and may not fit cleanly into categories. A trade loss could simultaneously have wrong timing AND poor execution AND an unexpected external shock.

**Risk:** Classification may be ambiguous, reducing the value of failure analysis.

### Assumption 4: Learning Transfers Between Domains

The Theory assumes that criterion developed in one domain (trading) transfers to other domains (creation, entrepreneurship). This is plausible but unproven. Trading has fast, unambiguous feedback. Creation has slower, more subjective feedback. The learning mechanisms that work in trading may not work in creation.

**Risk:** Criterion may be domain-specific. Each profile may need to develop its own criterion independently.

### Assumption 5: The System Can Self-Evaluate

The Constitution states that "the system should be able to evaluate its own decisions." This requires the system to have access to information about whether a decision was good independent of its outcome. But in complex systems, outcome and decision quality are often entangled.

**Risk:** Self-evaluation may be systematically biased. The system may rationalize good outcomes from bad decisions, or bad outcomes from good decisions.

### Assumption 6: More Evidence Always Improves Decisions

The philosophy assumes that accumulating evidence improves judgment. But in practice, more evidence can lead to overfitting, analysis paralysis, or confirmation bias. The system may need mechanisms to forget or deprioritize old evidence.

**Risk:** Evidence accumulation without evidence decay may degrade criterion rather than improve it.

## 3. Ideas That Still Require Validation

**The value of hypothesis tracking.** The theory states that explicit hypothesis tracking improves learning. This should be tested: does a system with hypothesis tracking learn faster than a system without it?

**The optimal balance of hypothesis diversity.** Too many hypotheses dilute evidence. Too few hypotheses miss opportunities. What is the right balance?

**The relationship between confidence and accuracy over time.** Does confidence calibration improve naturally with experience, or does it require explicit calibration mechanisms?

**Whether criterion can be measured independently of outcomes.** If criterion can only be measured by outcomes, it adds no information beyond existing metrics. A separate measurement approach is needed.

**The transferability of learning across domains.** This is the highest-risk assumption. It should be tested intentionally by running the system in different domains and measuring whether trading experience speeds up learning in creation or entrepreneurship.

**The shelf life of evidence.** How long does a piece of evidence remain valid? Does evidence from five years ago still inform today's decisions? Does it ever become misleading?

---

## 4. Living Theory Status

These documents should be treated as the beginning of a living scientific theory, not a finished philosophy.

They should be reviewed periodically (annually) and updated as evidence accumulates.

They should not be treated as sacred. If experience demonstrates that any principle is wrong, it should be changed.

The final measure of success is not whether the documents are elegant, but whether they guide the project toward better decisions over time.

## PART I: The Research Philosophy

### 1.1 Why Every Important Claim Must Eventually Become Evidence

An idea without evidence is opinion. An opinion is not worthless — it is a hypothesis waiting to be tested. But an opinion that is never tested becomes dogma.

The project distinguishes between three categories of claim:

| Category | Definition | Status | How It Changes |
|----------|-----------|--------|----------------|
| **Speculation** | An untested idea that may be true | Provisional — no confidence assigned | Becomes hypothesis when test is designed |
| **Hypothesis** | A testable claim with defined falsification conditions | Active — confidence assigned, evidence tracked | Becomes knowledge or is rejected through experiment |
| **Knowledge** | A hypothesis that has survived repeated testing | Provisional — confidence calibrated, still falsifiable | Replaced only when new evidence contradicts it |

No claim in this project should remain speculation indefinitely. Every claim must either become a testable hypothesis or be documented as an acknowledged unknown. Claims that cannot be tested must be labeled as such — they are philosophy, not science.

### 1.2 Why Ideas Are Temporary

Every idea in this project is a visitor. No idea is permanent. No concept is above revision. No principle is beyond challenge.

The half-life of an idea is determined by evidence, not by elegance. An elegant idea that fails its tests must leave. An ugly idea that survives repeated testing earns its place — until better evidence displaces it.

Ideas become permanent only through the slow accumulation of consistent, converging, independently verifiable evidence. Even then, they are not truly permanent. They are provisional with high confidence.

### 1.3 Why Theories Remain Provisional

A theory is a framework of interconnected hypotheses. It is the most efficient way to organize knowledge, but it is never the truth. It is a map of the territory — useful, simplifying, but always incomplete.

A theory that cannot be revised when new evidence arrives is not a theory. It is an ideology.

The project holds its theories provisionally. When evidence contradicts a theory, the theory yields — not the evidence. This is the defining commitment of the scientific mindset.

### 1.4 Why Evidence Has Authority over Intuition

Intuition is fast pattern recognition. It is valuable for generating hypotheses. It is dangerous for validating them.

Human intuition — and by extension, any system designed by humans — is systematically biased: overconfident, confirmation-biased, anchored to first impressions, influenced by irrelevant factors, and blind to its own limitations.

Evidence is the only correction mechanism. Evidence that contradicts intuition is not a problem to be explained away. It is a discovery to be learned from.

The project gives authority to evidence, not to the designer's intuition, not to the most eloquent document, not to the most elegant theory. Evidence has the final word because evidence is the only thing that connects the project's ideas to reality.

### 1.5 Why Disagreement Is Valuable

A research program with perfect agreement is a research program that has stopped learning. Disagreement reveals:
- Assumptions that were previously invisible
- Evidence that was ignored or undervalued
- Alternative hypotheses that were prematurely discarded
- Blind spots in the dominant framework

Disagreement is not conflict. It is the mechanism by which the project discovers what it does not know. The project should actively seek disagreement — through competing hypotheses, through red-team reviews, through explicit devil's advocacy.

A hypothesis that has survived genuine disagreement is stronger than one that has only been confirmed. Confirmation can be coincidence. Survival of genuine challenge is evidence.

### 1.6 Why Changing One's Mind Is a Strength

Changing one's mind in response to evidence is the defining characteristic of scientific thinking. It is not inconsistency. It is updating.

The project measures itself by the quality of its updates, not by the consistency of its past positions. A project that never changes its mind is a project that is not learning.

Every change of mind should be documented: what was believed, what evidence contradicted it, what the new position is, and why the evidence justifies the change. This documentation is itself evidence that the project can learn.

### 1.7 Why Intellectual Honesty Is a Competitive Advantage

Most projects selectively report results. They highlight successes, minimize failures, explain away contradictions, and present their strongest evidence while hiding their weaknesses. This creates an illusion of progress that breaks when exposed to reality.

Intellectual honesty — reporting failures as rigorously as successes, documenting assumptions as clearly as conclusions, acknowledging unknowns as explicitly as knowns — is a competitive advantage because:

- It accelerates error detection. Hidden errors compound. Visible errors get corrected.
- It builds trust. A project that is honest about its failures is more credible than one that hides them.
- It enables replication. Selective reporting makes replication impossible. Full reporting enables independent validation.
- It protects against overconfidence. Documenting what you do not know is the best defense against believing you know more than you do.

The project commits to intellectual honesty as its primary scientific value. Every result — positive, negative, inconclusive, ambiguous — is recorded. Every assumption is documented. Every unknown is acknowledged. This is not altruism. It is strategic.

### 2.2 Stage Descriptions

**Need:** The cycle begins with a gap in the project's understanding or capability. The system cannot judge something it needs to judge. The system cannot predict something it needs to predict. The system makes an error it does not understand. These gaps are needs. Without a need, there is no reason to conduct research. Research without a need is entertainment.

**Question:** The need is translated into a specific, answerable question. A good question is: specific enough to guide inquiry, answerable through observation or experiment, relevant to the need that triggered it. A bad question is: too broad to answer, too narrow to matter, or answerable only through speculation. The quality of the question determines the quality of everything that follows.

**Hypothesis:** The question generates a testable claim. The hypothesis must specify: what is expected, under what conditions, within what parameters, and what would falsify it. A hypothesis that cannot be falsified is not a research hypothesis — it is a belief statement. Multiple competing hypotheses should be generated for every significant question.

**Research:** Before designing an experiment, the project investigates what is already known. Has this question been answered elsewhere? Is there prior evidence within the project's own data? Are there competing theories that predict different outcomes? Research prevents reinvention and ensures the experiment tests something genuinely unknown.

**Design:** The experiment is designed. The design specifies: what data will be collected, what constitutes a positive result, what constitutes a negative result, what controls exist, what sample size is needed, what statistical tests will be used, what confounding factors must be controlled. Design is documented before the experiment begins — pre-registration prevents post-hoc rationalization.

**Experiment:** The experiment is executed according to the design. Deviations from the design are documented, not hidden. If the design proves flawed during execution, the experiment is stopped and redesigned — not continued with a flawed protocol.

**Observation:** Data is collected. Observations are recorded without interpretation at this stage. The raw observation is separated from its interpretation to prevent the observer from unconsciously biasing the record.

**Evidence:** Observations are interpreted relative to the hypothesis. The same observation may be evidence for one hypothesis and irrelevant to another. Evidence is labeled with direction (supports/contradicts), weight (how strong), and confidence (how reliable the interpretation is).

**Reflection:** The researcher steps back. Was the hypothesis confirmed? Was it rejected? Was the test fair? Were there confounding factors? What was learned beyond the original hypothesis? Reflection is the stage where outcome becomes understanding. Without reflection, data accumulates and knowledge does not.

**Knowledge:** A generalizable lesson is extracted. Knowledge is always provisional — it may be revised by future evidence. Knowledge is formulated as a statement that can be communicated, challenged, and tested independently.

**Criterion:** Knowledge updates the system's accumulated judgment. This update may be small (a slight calibration adjustment) or large (a new understanding of how a whole class of hypotheses works). Criterion is the integrated result of all previous cycles.

**Better Questions:** The cycle does not return to the same starting point. Each cycle produces questions that are more informed, more specific, more answerable than the previous cycle's questions. The trajectory of question quality over time is a measure of the project's research maturity.

### 2.3 Why the Loop Never Ends

The loop never ends because:

**The world changes.** What was true in one market regime may not hold in another. Knowledge that survives one environment must be revalidated in the next.

**Knowledge is never complete.** Every answer reveals new questions. A confirmed hypothesis narrows uncertainty but never eliminates it. The remaining uncertainty becomes the next need.

**Criterion is asymptotic.** It approaches a limit of perfect judgment but never reaches it. There is always room for better calibration, faster pattern recognition, more efficient resource allocation.

**Complacency is decay.** A system that stops researching because it believes it has learned enough will inevitably degrade as the world moves past it. The only stable state is active learning.

## PART III: Rules of Scientific Evolution

### 3.1 Permanent Rules

These rules are non-negotiable. They define how the project evolves. Any proposal that violates these rules must be rejected — no matter how elegant, how well-argued, or how strongly held.

---

**Rule 1 — No concept enters the core without evidence.**

A concept may be proposed, discussed, documented, and held as provisional. It becomes part of the project's core — the set of ideas that guide architecture, decisions, and evaluation — only when evidence supports it. The level of evidence required scales with the impact of the concept. A minor refinement requires less evidence than a foundational shift.

---

**Rule 2 — Every hypothesis must be falsifiable.**

A hypothesis must specify what evidence would disprove it. If a hypothesis cannot be falsified, it is not a research hypothesis. It may be a philosophical position, a value statement, or a design preference — but it must be labeled as such and kept separate from testable claims.

---

**Rule 3 — Every experiment requires a baseline.**

An experiment without a baseline cannot measure improvement. The baseline may be: random chance, the previous system version, a simplified alternative, human expert performance, or a theoretical optimum. The baseline must be specified before the experiment begins.

---

**Rule 4 — Every metric must have documented limitations.**

No metric is perfect. Every metric can be gamed, confounded, or misinterpreted. The limitations of each metric must be documented alongside the metric itself. A metric whose limitations are unknown is a metric whose results are untrustworthy.

---

**Rule 5 — Every improvement must be measurable.**

A claim of improvement must specify: the metric that improved, the baseline it improved from, the magnitude of improvement, the statistical significance, and the conditions under which the improvement was observed. An improvement that cannot be measured is not an improvement — it is a belief.

---

**Rule 6 — Every assumption must be documented.**

Every experiment, every design, every conclusion rests on assumptions. Assumptions must be documented before the results are known. Post-hoc assumption documentation is rationalization. Assumptions must include: what is assumed, why it is assumed, what evidence supports the assumption, and what would falsify it.

---

**Rule 7 — Every important failure must become knowledge.**

A failure that is not analyzed is a wasted experiment. Every failure that meets the importance threshold must trigger: error classification, root cause analysis, lesson extraction, and documentation. The lesson must be actionable — it must change how future experiments are designed or how future decisions are made.

---

**Rule 8 — Every rejected hypothesis must remain searchable.**

Rejected hypotheses are not deleted. They are archived with: the hypothesis statement, the evidence that rejected it, the conditions of the test, and any lessons extracted. A searchable archive of rejected hypotheses prevents the project from repeating experiments that have already been conducted. It also enables meta-analysis: what types of hypotheses tend to be rejected?

---

**Rule 9 — Every architectural change must explain why complexity is justified.**

The Constitution (Article 4) states: "Complexity Is Debt." Any proposal that adds complexity — new components, new layers, new abstractions, new dependencies — must include a complexity justification. The justification must: quantify the expected benefit, compare the proposed design to the simplest alternative, specify how the benefit will be measured, and define the conditions under which the complexity would be removed.

---

**Rule 10 — Every scientific claim must be challengeable.**

No claim in this project is above challenge. Any participant — human or automated — may challenge any scientific claim. The challenge must specify: what claim is being challenged, what evidence contradicts it, and what alternative explanation is proposed. The challenged claim must be defended with evidence, not authority. If the claim cannot be defended, it must be revised or retracted.

---

**Rule 11 — Results must be reproducible.**

An experiment whose results cannot be reproduced is not evidence. Reproducibility requires: the experiment design is documented in sufficient detail, the data is preserved, the analysis code or procedure is preserved, and the results do not depend on conditions that cannot be recreated.

---

**Rule 12 — The null hypothesis is always considered.**

Every experiment that claims improvement must consider the possibility that the improvement occurred by chance. The null hypothesis — "there is no improvement, any observed change is random" — must be explicitly stated and statistically evaluated. A claim that does not reject the null hypothesis is not evidence of improvement.

---

### 3.2 How Rules Change

These rules can be amended. Amendment requires:

1. A documented proposal explaining why the existing rule is inadequate
2. Evidence that the existing rule has produced harm (prevented a valid improvement, allowed an invalid one)
3. A specific replacement or modification
4. Ratification through review

Rules are not changed because they are inconvenient. They are changed only when evidence demonstrates they are wrong.

## PART IV: Levels of Evidence

### 4.1 The Evidence Hierarchy

Not all evidence is equal. The project uses a seven-level hierarchy to classify evidence quality. Higher levels provide stronger support for claims and are required for more consequential decisions.

| Level | Name | Definition | Example | Minimum for |
|-------|------|-----------|---------|-------------|
| **L0** | Opinion | An untested belief, intuition, or preference | "I think this approach will work" | Generating hypotheses |
| **L1** | Observation | A single instance of something happening, without controlled conditions | "The system made a profit on this trade" | Identifying needs, generating questions |
| **L2** | Repeated Observation | Multiple instances showing a pattern, but without controls | "The system has been profitable for 10 consecutive trades" | Detecting patterns worth investigating |
| **L3** | Controlled Experiment | A test comparing the hypothesis against a defined baseline, controlling for confounds | "The hypothesis lifecycle version outperformed the baseline version by 12% in a 90-day A/B test (p < 0.05)" | Claiming an improvement works |
| **L4** | Historical Validation | Evidence that the improvement persists across multiple historical periods, tested out-of-sample | "The improvement holds across 3 years of historical data spanning bull, bear, and sideways markets" | Claiming robustness |
| **L5** | Cross-Domain Validation | Evidence that the same mechanism produces improvement in multiple domains | "The hypothesis lifecycle improved decisions in trading, then replicated in prediction markets and creator analytics" | Claiming domain-independence |
| **L6** | Longitudinal Validation | Evidence that the improvement persists and compounds over years of real-time operation | "Criterion metrics improved continuously over 5 years of live operation across 4 market regimes" | Claiming criterion development |

### 4.2 Evidence Requirements by Decision Type

| Decision Type | Minimum Evidence Level | Rationale |
|---------------|----------------------|-----------|
| Add a new metric to the Criterion Score | L2 (Repeated Observation) | Metrics are observational; they can be refined |
| Add a new hypothesis state | L2 (Repeated Observation) | State transitions are design decisions with limited risk |
| Change an evidence weighting formula | L3 (Controlled Experiment) | Weighting affects decisions; requires controlled validation |
| Add a new architectural component | L3 (Controlled Experiment) | Complexity requires evidence of benefit |
| Change the hypothesis lifecycle | L3 (Controlled Experiment) | Core mechanism; must demonstrate improvement over baseline |
| Claim criterion is improving | L4 (Historical Validation) | Requires persistence across conditions |
| Claim transfer to new domain | L5 (Cross-Domain Validation) | Must be demonstrated, not assumed |
| Claim the project has developed operational criterion | L6 (Longitudinal Validation) | Requires years of demonstrated improvement |

### 4.3 Evidence Quality Dimensions

Beyond the hierarchy level, evidence quality is evaluated along these dimensions:

**Relevance:** Does the evidence directly address the hypothesis? Or is it tangential?

**Independence:** Is the evidence from multiple independent sources? Correlated sources do not multiply confidence.

**Specificity:** Is the evidence precise? Vague evidence ("the system seems better") is weaker than specific evidence ("calibration improved from ±18pp to ±11pp").

**Temporal order:** Did the evidence exist before the hypothesis, or was it collected after? Post-hoc evidence is weaker because it can be selected to fit the hypothesis.

**Sample size:** How many observations support the claim? Single observations are anecdotes, not evidence.

**Effect size:** How large is the observed effect? A 1% improvement requires more evidence than a 20% improvement.

**Statistical significance:** Could the observation be explained by random chance? Statistical tests provide a formal answer.

**Reproducibility:** Has the result been independently reproduced? A single successful experiment is a data point. A reproducible result is evidence.

## PART V: Research Quality

### 5.1 What Makes Research Good

Good research in O.M.A.-C.O.R.E. is defined by these characteristics:

**Clear Question.** The research addresses a specific, well-defined question. The question connects to a genuine need — a gap in the project's criterion, not idle curiosity.

**Explicit Assumptions.** Every assumption underlying the research is documented before the results are known. Assumptions include: what the hypothesis assumes about the world, what the experiment design assumes about the system, what the metrics assume about measurement.

**Alternative Explanations.** The research considers alternative explanations for the expected results. If the hypothesis is confirmed, what else could explain the observation? If it is rejected, what else could explain the failure?

**Competing Hypotheses.** For significant questions, multiple competing hypotheses are generated and tested against the same evidence. A single hypothesis tested in isolation provides less information than the same evidence evaluated against alternatives.

**Reproducibility.** The experiment can be repeated by an independent observer with the same results. This requires: documented design, preserved data, preserved analysis code, and specified conditions.

**Falsifiability.** The hypothesis specifies what evidence would disprove it. Research that cannot fail is not research — it is confirmation-seeking.

**Calibration.** The research's confidence claims match its actual track record. If the project is 70% confident in a hypothesis, it should be right approximately 70% of the time across many hypotheses.

**Transparency.** All results are reported — positive, negative, inconclusive, ambiguous. Selective reporting is the most common form of scientific malpractice and is explicitly prohibited.

**Scientific Humility.** The research acknowledges its limitations. It does not claim more than the evidence supports. It identifies what remains unknown and what future research is needed.

### 5.2 How Low-Quality Research Damages Criterion

Low-quality research is not neutral. It actively damages the project's criterion because:

**False positives train superstition.** A claim that appears to be confirmed but is actually noise teaches the system to rely on unreliable patterns. Each false positive degrades calibration.

**False negatives waste knowledge.** A claim that is rejected due to poor experimental design may actually be valid. The system discards a potentially useful hypothesis because the test was flawed.

**Confirmation bias is self-reinforcing.** Research designed to confirm rather than test produces a stream of false confirmations that make the project increasingly confident in increasingly wrong beliefs.

**Hindsight bias distorts evaluation.** Research that evaluates decisions by their outcomes rather than by the evidence available at the time produces systematic overconfidence in lucky decisions and underconfidence in unlucky ones.

**Measurement artifacts become targets.** When flawed metrics are used to evaluate research, the project optimizes for the metric rather than for the underlying capability. This produces apparent improvement and actual degradation.

### 5.3 Research Purity

Some research is better than no research. But research with known flaws should be labeled accordingly. The project uses three quality labels:

| Label | Meaning | Use |
|-------|---------|-----|
| **Exploratory** | Preliminary investigation, uncontrolled, small sample | Generating hypotheses, identifying patterns |
| **Substantive** | Controlled experiment, adequate sample, documented design | Testing specific hypotheses |
| **Confirmatory** | Independent replication, across conditions, pre-registered | Validating knowledge claims |

All research should be labeled. An exploratory study is not held to the same standard as a confirmatory study, but it must not be presented as if it were one.

## PART VI: Failure Intelligence

### 6.1 Failure Is a Scientific Asset

Failure is not the opposite of success in a research program. Failure is evidence that the project's understanding was incomplete. Every failure reveals a gap between the project's model of reality and reality itself.

A project that never fails is a project that is not testing its boundaries. It is operating within its comfort zone, confirming what it already knows.

A project that fails and learns is a project that expands its boundaries.

### 6.2 Types of Negative Outcomes

Not all negative outcomes are the same. The project distinguishes:

| Type | Definition | Example | Response |
|------|-----------|---------|----------|
| **Failure** | A hypothesis was tested and conclusively rejected. The prediction was wrong. | "Hypothesis predicted gold would rise 2-5% in 10 days. Gold dropped 3%." | Classify error, extract lesson, update priors. |
| **Mistake** | An error in the research process, not in the hypothesis. The design, execution, or analysis was flawed. | "The experiment used incorrect data. The test was invalid." | Correct the process, do not use the result as evidence about the hypothesis. |
| **Noise** | The outcome was within the expected random variation. Neither confirmed nor rejected. | "The result was 1% above baseline, p = 0.34. Null hypothesis not rejected." | Do not interpret. Increase sample size or improve experimental sensitivity. |
| **Randomness** | The outcome was dominated by factors outside the hypothesis and outside the system's control. | "An unexpected regulatory announcement reversed the market during the test window." | Document the external factor. Do not treat the outcome as evidence about the hypothesis. |
| **Unknown Unknown** | An outcome that reveals a factor the project did not know existed. | "The result contradicted all hypotheses. Investigation revealed a previously unknown correlation between two event types." | This is the most valuable type. It expands the project's understanding of what it does not know. |

### 6.3 The Failure Intelligence Cycle

Every important failure should trigger:

```
Failure Observed
       │
       ▼
Classification ──── What type of negative outcome is this?
       │
       ▼
Root Cause ───────── Why did it happen? Was the hypothesis wrong?
       │              Was the evidence insufficient? Was the design flawed?
       ▼
Lesson Extraction ─── What does this teach us? Is the lesson generalizable?
       │
       ▼
Knowledge Update ──── How does this change what we know?
       │
       ▼
Criterion Update ──── How does this change the way we judge?
       │
       ▼
Process Change ────── What should we do differently next time?
```

### 6.4 Why Failures Must Be Preserved

A deleted failure is a lesson that cannot be learned. The project preserves all significant failures in a searchable, queryable archive. The archive is not a record of shame — it is a record of learning.

The failure archive enables:
- Meta-analysis: What types of hypotheses fail most often?
- Pattern detection: Do failures cluster in certain conditions?
- Learning measurement: Are failure frequencies decreasing over time?
- Calibration assessment: Are rejected hypotheses consistent with the system's confidence at the time of decision?

A project that deletes its failures is a project that cannot measure whether it is learning.

## PART VII: The Evolution of Criterion

### 7.1 Criterion Is Never Complete

Criterion is not a destination. It is a direction.

The project will never achieve perfect judgment. The world is too complex, too stochastic, too full of hidden variables. Every time the system learns something, the world reveals something new to be learned.

This is not a limitation. It is the nature of operating under uncertainty. The project's goal is not to reach a final state of perfect criterion. Its goal is to maintain a positive trajectory of criterion improvement over time.

### 7.2 The Asymptotic Trajectory

Criterion approaches a theoretical limit but never reaches it:

```
Criterion
  ▲
  │                                            asymptote (perfect judgment)
  │                                          .•´
  │                                       .•´
  │                                    .•´
  │                                 .•´
  │                              .•´
  │                          .•´
  │                      .•´
  │                 .•´
  │            .•´
  │       .•´
  │  .•´
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────► Time (years)
```

Each research cycle produces a small increment. Early increments are large (the system learns basic patterns). Later increments are smaller (refining already good calibration). But the system never stops improving because:
- The world changes, creating new things to learn
- The system's improving criterion allows it to detect subtler patterns it previously could not see
- Each improvement reveals the next limitation

### 7.3 What Criterion Improvement Looks Like

Over cycles, the system should:

**Ask better questions.** Questions become more specific, more answerable, more decision-relevant. The system stops asking "What will happen?" and starts asking "Under what conditions does this pattern hold?"

**Generate better hypotheses.** Hypotheses become more precise, more falsifiable, more grounded in evidence. The system stops generating vague beliefs and starts generating testable predictions.

**Need less irrelevant information.** The system learns what information is predictive and what is noise. It collects less noise and more signal over time.

**Calibrate confidence more accurately.** Confidence estimates converge toward actual outcome frequencies. The system becomes neither overconfident (hubris) nor underconfident (paralysis).

**Recognize stronger patterns.** Patterns become more context-specific, more precisely quantified, and more robust. The system moves from "this pattern works" to "this pattern works in these conditions with this reliability."

**Reduce uncertainty faster.** The system requires less evidence to reach the same confidence level. It has seen enough similar situations to recognize them quickly.

**Allocate resources more intelligently.** Capital, attention, and time are directed toward the most promising hypotheses and away from the least promising ones. The system makes fewer low-value bets.

**Know when not to act.** The most advanced form of criterion is knowing when the evidence is insufficient, when the stakes are too high, and when waiting is the correct decision.

### 7.4 The Plateau Risk

Criterion improvement is not guaranteed to be monotonic. Plateaus are expected — periods where no measurable improvement occurs despite continued research.

A plateau is not failure. It is information. It tells the project that:
- The current research approach may have reached its limit
- A different type of research may be needed
- The system may have reached the ceiling of its current architecture
- The world may have changed in a way that invalidates previous learning

The response to a plateau is investigation, not acceleration. The project asks: Why have we stopped improving? What is the bottleneck? What kind of research would break through this plateau?

## PART VIII: The Role of the MVP

### 8.1 The MVP Is the Laboratory

The MVP is not the product. It is not the destination. It is the experimental apparatus — the laboratory in which the project tests its hypotheses about criterion development.

A laboratory is judged by the quality of experiments it enables, not by the profitability of its operations. The MVP's purpose is to produce evidence, not profits. Profits, when they occur, are consequences of validated criterion — not validation themselves.

### 8.2 Trading Is the Current Experimental Domain

Trading is the first domain because it provides:
- Fast feedback (hours to days)
- Unambiguous outcomes (profit/loss is measurable)
- Abundant data (thousands of cycles possible)
- Clear consequence chains (event → hypothesis → action → outcome is observable)
- Objective measurement (no subjective interpretation of outcomes)

Trading is an experimental domain, not an identity. The project is not a trading system that happens to have a philosophy. It is a scientific research program that uses trading as its first experimental environment because trading provides the most favorable conditions for developing and validating the criterion framework.

### 8.3 Every Trade Is an Experiment

A trade is never just a trade. It is an experiment that tests a hypothesis. Every trade must be linked to the hypothesis it tests, the evidence that supported it, the confidence at decision time, and the outcome that resulted.

A trade without a hypothesis is not an experiment. It is noise. It produces a data point but no knowledge.

### 8.4 Every Hypothesis Tested Strengthens or Weakens the Theory

Every hypothesis lifecycle — from creation through evidence collection, testing, outcome, reflection, and knowledge extraction — either strengthens the project's theory (if confirmed under rigorous conditions) or weakens it (if rejected).

This is the mechanism by which the project learns. Each hypothesis-outcome cycle is a single data point. Over hundreds of cycles, patterns emerge. Over thousands of cycles, the theory is validated, refined, or abandoned.

### 8.5 The MVP Produces Evidence, Not Profits

The project evaluates the MVP by the evidence it produces, not by the profits it generates. The questions that matter are:
- How many hypotheses were tested?
- What was the outcome of each test?
- What was learned from each outcome?
- Did hypothesis quality improve over time?
- Did confidence calibration improve?
- Did error type frequencies decline?

Profit questions are secondary. They matter only insofar as they provide evidence about criterion development. A profitable system that is not learning is failing. An unprofitable system that is learning is succeeding — it is generating the evidence needed to improve.

## PART IX: The Scientific Responsibility of the Project

### 9.1 Ethical Commitments

**Never fabricate evidence.** Results must reflect what actually happened, not what the project wishes had happened. Fabrication is the only unforgivable scientific crime.

**Never hide failures.** Failures are assets. They are preserved, analyzed, and learned from. Hiding a failure is stealing a learning opportunity from the project's future self.

**Never optimize metrics while degrading reasoning.** When a metric becomes a target, it ceases to be a good metric. The project must guard against Goodhart's Law by maintaining multiple metrics and periodically evaluating whether metric improvement corresponds to genuine improvement.

**Never confuse confidence with certainty.** Confidence is a calibrated estimate, not a guarantee. The project must never present its conclusions as certain. Every claim must carry an explicit expression of uncertainty.

**Never confuse correlation with causation.** Two things that happen together are not necessarily causally related. The project requires causal evidence — experimental manipulation, temporal ordering, mechanism identification — before claiming causation.

**Never preserve ideas because they are elegant.** Elegance is a reason to investigate an idea. It is not a reason to keep it. Ideas survive on evidence, not aesthetics.

**Never reject evidence because it is inconvenient.** Evidence that contradicts the project's preferred conclusions is the most valuable evidence. It reveals what the project would not otherwise discover.

### 9.2 Scientific Integrity in Practice

**Document before the fact.** Hypotheses, designs, and assumptions are documented before experiments begin. Post-hoc documentation is rationalization.

**Report all results.** Results that contradict expectations are reported with the same rigor and visibility as results that confirm them.

**Share data internally.** All experimental data is accessible to all project participants, unless privacy or security constraints prevent it.

**Maintain an archive of rejected ideas.** Rejected hypotheses, failed experiments, and discarded designs remain searchable. They are not deleted or hidden.

**Review periodically.** The project's scientific practices are reviewed annually. The review examines: Are we following our own protocol? Are we hiding failures? Are we preserving rejected ideas? Are we letting elegance override evidence?

## Self-Review

### S-01: Weak Assumptions

**Assumption: Research protocol improves research outcomes.**
This document assumes that a formal research protocol produces better science than informal exploration. This is supported by the history of science (systematic methods outperform intuition), but it is not proven for this specific project. The protocol may create overhead that slows learning without improving quality. This should be tested: compare a period with strict protocol adherence against a period with loose adherence.

**Assumption: The research loop is complete.**
The 12-stage loop (Need through Better Questions) may omit stages that matter. Iteration is mentioned but not formalized. The loop may need a "Return to Need" transition for cases where the experiment failed to answer the question.

**Assumption: All researchers can follow this protocol consistently.**
If the project has human participants, protocol adherence will vary. If the project is fully automated, the protocol must be executable by code. Neither assumption is validated.

### S-02: Scientific Weaknesses

**The protocol cannot prevent self-deception.**
A determined researcher can follow every rule and still deceive themselves — by asking leading questions, designing biased experiments, interpreting ambiguous results favorably. The protocol reduces this risk but cannot eliminate it. The best defense is a culture that rewards intellectual honesty.

**Evidence levels are approximations.**
The seven-level hierarchy (L0–L6) implies a linear progression that does not exist in practice. Some knowledge is best supported by converging evidence across multiple lower-level studies rather than a single higher-level study. The hierarchy is a heuristic, not a law.

**The falsification requirement is strict.**
Some valuable hypotheses are difficult to falsify precisely. A claim like "criterion improves decision quality" can only be falsified through longitudinal observation, which takes years. The project must distinguish between "not yet falsifiable" and "not falsifiable in principle."

### S-03: Research Risks

**Risk: Bureaucratic overhead.**
If the protocol is applied too rigidly, it may slow research to the point where the project cannot keep pace with the changing environment. The protocol should be applied proportionally — lightweight for exploratory research, rigorous for confirmatory research.

**Risk: Protocol worship.**
The protocol may become an end in itself. Researchers may follow the rules mechanically without understanding their purpose. Periodic reviews should examine whether the protocol is serving the research or the research is serving the protocol.

**Risk: False security.**
A project that follows this protocol may become overconfident in its results. "We followed the protocol, so the result must be valid." Protocol adherence improves reliability but does not guarantee correctness.

**Risk: The protocol becomes dogma.**
This document states that every claim is provisional — including the claims in this document. But over time, the protocol itself may become unchallengeable. The project must periodically challenge its own research rules.

### S-04: Biases That May Still Exist

**Survivorship bias in retrospectives.**
When reviewing past research, the project may focus on successful experiments and forget failed ones. The archive of failures protects against this, but only if it is actively consulted.

**Publication bias in reporting.**
The project may unconsciously report positive results more thoroughly than negative ones. The requirement to report all results protects against this, but only if enforced.

**Confirmation bias in interpretation.**
Given ambiguous results, the project may lean toward interpretations that confirm existing beliefs. The competing hypothesis requirement protects against this, but only if competing hypotheses are genuinely different (not straw men).

### S-05: Blind Spots

**The protocol does not address resource allocation.**
How does the project decide which research questions to pursue? The protocol begins with "Need" but does not define how needs are prioritized among competing demands.

**The protocol does not define who decides.**
Who determines whether evidence is sufficient? Who decides when a hypothesis is confirmed? Who enforces protocol adherence? These governance questions are outside the protocol's scope but essential for its operation.

**The protocol assumes rational actors.**
It assumes that everyone involved will follow the rules, report honestly, and value evidence over ego. In practice, these are cultural achievements, not assumptions.

### S-06: How the Protocol Could Fail

**The protocol could produce dogma.**
If followed rigidly, the protocol could prevent the project from adopting ideas that lack formal evidence but represent genuine advances. The protocol must allow for rapid, informal hypothesis testing.

**The protocol could slow learning.**
If every hypothesis requires a full research cycle, the project may test fewer hypotheses than an informal approach. The protocol should offer a fast path for low-risk, high-potential ideas.

**The protocol could be ignored.**
If the protocol is too burdensome, it will be bypassed. The protocol must be lightweight enough to be followed consistently.

**The protocol could become irrelevant.**
As the project evolves, the research challenges will change. The protocol must evolve with them. A protocol that never changes is a protocol that has become dogma.

### S-07: Proposed Improvements

1. **Add a "Fast Path" for low-stakes hypotheses.** Not every hypothesis requires a full research cycle. Define criteria for fast-path research (exploratory, low risk, high potential) and full-path research (confirmatory, high impact, foundational).

2. **Add a governance section.** Who makes decisions about protocol interpretation, evidence sufficiency, and research priorities? This should be documented, not assumed.

3. **Add a periodic protocol review.** Every 12 months, the protocol itself should be reviewed against the project's experience. What worked? What was burdensome? What was bypassed?

4. **Add explicit criteria for "sufficient evidence."** The protocol defines levels but not thresholds. When does evidence move a concept from L3 (controlled experiment) to L4 (historical validation)? The thresholds should be defined per concept type.

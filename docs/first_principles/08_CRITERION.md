# Criterion

## The Emergent Capability

Criterion is the central concept of O.M.A.-C.O.R.E. It is also the most easily misunderstood.

This document exists to prevent that misunderstanding.

---

## What Criterion Is Not

Criterion is not an engine. There will never be a `CriterionEngine.py` file whose methods are called in a pipeline. Criterion is not a component that can be built, installed, or deployed.

Criterion is not an object. It is not a class with attributes and methods. It is not a data structure that can be serialized and stored.

Criterion is not a database table. It is not a set of rows with timestamps and values. It is not something that can be queried with SQL.

Criterion is not an AI model. It is not a neural network, a transformer, or any other machine learning architecture. It is not something that can be trained, fine-tuned, or deployed to an inference server.

**Criterion is an emergent capability.**

It arises from the interaction of many components over time, none of which individually is criterion. It is to O.M.A.-C.O.R.E. what intelligence is to a brain — not a part, but a property of the whole.

---

## What Criterion Is

Criterion is the system's accumulated ability to judge what matters.

It manifests in the system's decisions, but it is not reducible to any single decision. It is the pattern behind the decisions — the consistent ability to:

- Recognize which consequences are forming before they become obvious
- Estimate how much evidence is enough to act
- Distinguish between signal and noise in context
- Calibrate confidence to match actual accuracy
- Identify when a hypothesis has been invalidated
- Learn from outcomes without overfitting to them
- Apply lessons across different contexts
- Improve over time without forgetting

Criterion is the output of the system's learning process. It is what remains after thousands of hypotheses have been tested, thousands of outcomes have been observed, and thousands of errors have been classified.

---

## What Produces Criterion

Criterion emerges from the interaction of:

**Memory** — persistent storage of hypotheses, decisions, outcomes, evidence, and errors. Without memory, every cycle starts from zero. Criterion cannot develop.

**Learning** — the process of extracting patterns from memory. Learning transforms outcomes into knowledge. Without learning, memory is just storage.

**Hypotheses** — structured beliefs that connect events to consequences. Hypotheses are the units of learning. Each hypothesis is a test. Each outcome is evidence.

**Evidence** — data that supports or contradicts hypotheses. Evidence is the raw material from which confidence is built and criterion is refined.

**Outcomes** — the results of actions. Outcomes are the feedback signal. Without outcomes, there is no way to distinguish good hypotheses from bad ones.

**Knowledge** — patterns extracted from outcomes. Knowledge is what the system knows about how the world works. It is always provisional.

**Context** — the conditions under which hypotheses are formed and tested. Context determines whether knowledge applies to the current situation.

**Time** — the dimension in which criterion develops. Criterion is not acquired instantly. It requires repeated cycles of hypothesis, action, outcome, and learning. There are no shortcuts.

## Criterion Levels (Conceptual)

These levels describe possible stages of criterion development. They are theoretical — not implementation targets.

### Level 0 — Reactive

The system responds to events as they arrive. No memory. No learning. No hypotheses. Every decision is a reflex based on current inputs.

**Behavior:** Reacts to everything. Overwhelmed by noise.

### Level 1 — Pattern Recognition

The system detects recurring patterns but cannot form or test hypotheses about them. It recognizes "this looks like the pattern before the last crash" but cannot explain why.

**Behavior:** Better than reactive, but brittle. Patterns that change break the system.

### Level 2 — Contextual

The system understands that patterns depend on context. A pattern that works in a bull market may not work in a bear market. The system can adjust its behavior based on regime.

**Behavior:** More robust than pattern recognition. Adapts to changing conditions within known contexts.

### Level 3 — Hypothesis-Driven

The system forms explicit hypotheses about consequences, tracks evidence for and against each hypothesis, and updates them as new information arrives. Decisions are traceable to hypotheses.

**Behavior:** Capable of explaining its decisions. Can identify which assumptions were correct and which were wrong.

### Level 4 — Self-Correcting

The system detects when its own hypotheses are incorrect and adjusts before the outcome confirms the error. It identifies weakening evidence and reduces position size or exits before the loss.

**Behavior:** Proactive error correction. Does not wait for outcomes to invalidate hypotheses — detects invalidation as it emerges.

### Level 5 — Operational Criterion

The system consistently demonstrates calibrated judgment across multiple domains and conditions. Confidence matches accuracy. Hypothesis quality improves over time. The system learns from every outcome.

**Behavior:** Reliable judgment. Consistent improvement. Able to operate in novel conditions using accumulated principles rather than memorized patterns.

### Level 6 — Strategic Criterion

The system operates at a strategic level — not just identifying which trade to execute, but which domains to operate in, which questions to investigate, and which capabilities to develop. It can evaluate its own trajectory.

**Behavior:** Self-directed improvement. The system not only learns — it decides what to learn next.

## Measuring Criterion

Criterion cannot be measured directly, but it can be inferred from behavior:

- **Calibration** — Does the system's confidence match its accuracy? If it is 70% confident, is it right 70% of the time?
- **Error classification** — Does the system repeat the same type of error, or does error frequency decrease over time?
- **Hypothesis quality** — Do hypotheses become more precise, more testable, and more accurate over time?
- **Adaptation speed** — How quickly does the system adjust when conditions change?
- **Knowledge persistence** — Does the system retain lessons across different contexts, or does it forget after regime changes?
- **Decision traceability** — Can the system explain why it made a decision, and was the explanation consistent with the evidence available at the time?

---

## What Criterion Requires

Criterion requires time. It cannot be accelerated. The system must go through enough hypothesis-outcome cycles to accumulate the evidence that criterion requires.

Criterion requires honesty. Errors must be preserved and classified, not hidden or ignored. A system that cannot acknowledge its failures cannot develop criterion.

Criterion requires structure. Memory must be organized so that patterns can be detected. Outcomes must be linked to hypotheses. Evidence must be tracked across time.

Criterion requires patience. The system will make errors. It will hold hypotheses that turn out to be wrong. This is not failure. This is the process.

---

## What Criterion Does Not Require

Criterion does not require a large model. A system with a hundred well-structured hypotheses and outcomes can develop more criterion than a system with a million data points and no structure.

Criterion does not require real-time data. It develops from the quality of learning, not the speed of data ingestion.

Criterion does not require domain expertise. It develops from repeated cycles of hypothesis, action, outcome, and learning. The domain provides the feedback — the learning process is domain-independent.

---

## Criterion as the Long-Term Measure

In the early stages of O.M.A.-C.O.R.E., the system will be evaluated by conventional metrics: win rate, PnL, Sharpe ratio. These are necessary for validation but not sufficient for guidance.

As the system matures, criterion should become the primary measure of system health. The question should shift from "is the system profitable?" to "is the system developing criterion?"

A profitable system that is not developing criterion is a system that is lucky but not learning. A break-even system that is developing criterion is a system that is investing in future capability.

The ultimate validation of O.M.A.-C.O.R.E. will not be a profitable quarter. It will be a system that, after ten years, demonstrates better judgment than it did after one year — and can explain exactly what it has learned along the way.

# Project Constitution

*The foundational commitments that define how O.M.A.-C.O.R.E. is built, evaluated, and evolved.*

---

## Article 1 — Project Values

### 1.1 Evidence Supremacy

Evidence is the highest authority in this project. No idea, no architecture, no model, no algorithm is above evidence. When evidence contradicts a position, the position yields.

### 1.2 Intellectual Honesty

The system should be as honest about its failures as about its successes. Errors should be classified, preserved, and learned from — not hidden, excused, or deleted.

### 1.3 Long-Term Thinking

Decisions should be evaluated over years, not days. Short-term optimization at the expense of long-term capability is not optimization — it is debt.

### 1.4 Humility

The system's understanding of the world is always incomplete. Every hypothesis is provisional. Every conclusion is subject to revision. Certainty is not a virtue — it is a warning sign.

### 1.5 Independence from Technology

The project's philosophy must survive any technology migration. The principles should be implementable in any language, on any platform, with any AI framework. If a technology choice constrains the philosophy, the technology should be replaced.

---

## Article 2 — Decision Process

### 2.1 Hypothesis-First

Every decision should be framed as a hypothesis: "We believe that X will produce Y, based on evidence Z." The hypothesis should be testable. If it cannot be tested, it is not ready for implementation.

### 2.2 Evidence Required

Decisions should be supported by evidence. The evidence may be weak — early-stage projects have limited data. But the evidence should be explicit, and its limitations should be acknowledged.

### 2.3 Falsifiability

Every hypothesis about the system should specify what would falsify it. If a hypothesis cannot be falsified, it cannot be learned from.

### 2.4 Consent of the Measured

The system should be able to evaluate its own decisions. If a decision cannot be evaluated, it should not have been made.

---

## Article 3 — Evidence-First Culture

### 3.1 Assertions Require Evidence

Any claim about system behavior, performance, or capability must be accompanied by evidence. The quality of the evidence should match the strength of the claim.

### 3.2 Negative Results Are Results

A hypothesis that is invalidated is as valuable as a hypothesis that is validated. Both produce evidence. Both improve criterion. Negative results should be recorded with the same rigor as positive ones.

### 3.3 Replication Is Validation

A result that cannot be replicated across different conditions is not yet knowledge. It is an observation. The system should track which results replicate and which do not.

---

## Article 4 — Simplicity

### 4.1 Complexity Is Debt

Every component, every layer, every abstraction is a liability until proven otherwise. Complexity should be justified by evidence of benefit, not assumed.

### 4.2 Default to No

The default answer to any proposed addition should be "no." The proposer must demonstrate how the addition serves the project's principles.

### 4.3 Removal Is Improvement

Removing a component that no longer justifies its complexity is a contribution, not a loss. The system should be edited as aggressively as it is extended.

---

## Article 5 — Long-Term Thinking

### 5.1 The Ten-Year Test

Any architecture decision should be evaluated against the question: "Will this still make sense in ten years?" If the answer is unclear, the decision should be simplified.

### 5.2 Technology Agnosticism

No dependency on a specific AI model, programming language, or platform should become irreplaceable. The ability to migrate should always exist.

### 5.3 Knowledge Persistence

Knowledge developed by the system — hypotheses, outcomes, evidence, criterion — should survive any technology change. The database of experience is more valuable than any single implementation.

---

## Article 6 — Scientific Mindset

### 6.1 Everything Is Provisional

No conclusion in this project is final. Every finding is subject to revision when new evidence accumulates. The project should maintain a running list of open questions and unresolved hypotheses.

### 6.2 Peer Review

Decisions and findings should be reviewable by anyone who examines the evidence. The system's reasoning should be transparent enough that an independent observer can evaluate whether a decision was justified.

### 6.3 Continuous Refinement

The project should improve continuously, not in bursts. Small, evidence-based refinements are preferred over large, untested redesigns.

---

## Article 7 — Humility

### 7.1 The System Does Not Know

The system should never represent its conclusions as certain. Confidence should be explicit, calibrated, and accompanied by evidence.

### 7.2 Unknowns Should Be Acknowledged

What the system does not know is as important as what it knows. Unknowns should be tracked, and the system should be aware of the limits of its own knowledge.

### 7.3 The World Is Complex

No model captures the full complexity of the world. The system's representations are simplifications. They are useful, but they are not the truth.

---

## Article 8 — Amendment

### 8.1 This Constitution Can Be Changed

If evidence accumulates that any principle in this constitution is wrong or incomplete, it should be amended. The constitution is not a straitjacket — it is a record of current understanding.

### 8.2 Amendments Require Extraordinary Evidence

Because the constitution defines the project's foundations, amending it requires stronger evidence than ordinary decisions. The evidence should be documented and the reasoning should be explicit.

### 8.3 Amendments Must Be Ratified

Constitutional amendments should be reviewed by multiple perspectives before adoption. The review should examine whether the amendment improves the project's long-term trajectory.

---

*No future contributor should violate these principles without extraordinary justification. If such justification exists, it should be documented as an amendment to this constitution.*

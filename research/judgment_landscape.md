# Landscape Scan: Expert Judgment Under Uncertainty

**Date:** 2026-06-25
**Topic:** How the world's best decision makers develop judgment under uncertainty
**Route:** B (Focused Search)

---

## Key Findings from Landscape Scan

### 1. Naturalistic Decision Making (NDM) — Gary Klein
- Field that examines how experienced practitioners actually decide, as opposed to rational-choice models.
- Key domains: military commanders, firefighters, ICU nurses, chess grandmasters.
- Recognition-Primed Decision (RPD) model: System 1 pattern recognition generates initial response; System 2 mental simulation evaluates it.
- Experts recognize patterns, not options. First instinct is usually right because they've seen similar situations thousands of times.
- Pre-mortem technique: imagining failure has already occurred, then working backward to find causes. Increases identification of failure reasons by ~30%.

### 2. Dual-Process Theory — Kahneman & Tversky
- System 1: fast, intuitive, effortless (pattern recognition).
- System 2: slow, deliberate, effortful (analytical evaluation).
- Expert intuition is disciplined System 1 — pattern recognition operating at speed.
- Conditions for valid expert intuition: (1) sufficiently regular/predictable environment, (2) adequate opportunity to practice with timely, accurate feedback.
- Low-validity environments (stock markets, political forecasting): even experienced practitioners unlikely to build skilled intuition.

### 3. Deliberate Practice — K.A. Ericsson
- Expertise is not just hours; it's deliberate practice with specific goals, feedback, and reflection.
- The "10,000-hour rule" is insufficient alone — need quality of practice, not just quantity.
- Path: novice → intermediate (journeyman) → expert. Post-instruction performance peaks after ~2 years at acceptable level; true expertise requires substantially more.
- Experts accumulate 50,000–100,000 domain-specific chunks/patterns.

### 4. After-Action Reviews (AARs) & Debriefing Culture
- Military origin, spread to aviation, medicine, emergency response.
- Focus on learning, not blame. Structured around: (1) what was intended, (2) what actually happened, (3) why the gap, (4) how to improve.
- Post-fall huddles in hospitals: adoption increased over time; reduction in severe outcomes; task errors decreased but judgment errors harder to identify accurately.
- Debriefing evolved from military post-mission analysis → aviation → anesthesiology → broader healthcare.

### 5. Cognitive Bias Mitigation in Intelligence & Military
- Red teams: independent capability to conduct critical reviews from alternative perspectives.
- Devil's advocacy: challenging prevailing views to expose faulty reasoning and assumptions.
- Analysis of Competing Hypotheses (ACH): Richard Heuer's structured technique to mitigate confirmation bias.
- Key problem: "There is no subjective marker that distinguishes correct intuitions from those produced by highly imperfect heuristics."
- Kahneman: "I have more confidence in the ability of institutions to improve their thinking than in the ability of individuals."

### 6. Confidence Calibration — Dunning-Kruger & Metacognition
- Novices dramatically overestimate competence; top performers slightly underestimate (project competence onto peers).
- In surgery: stages of euphoria → valley of humility → enlightenment → wisdom.
- True expertise correlates with calibrated humility, not swagger.
- Metacognitive awareness (ability to monitor/evaluate one's own decisions) is equally critical as technical skill.

### 7. Systematization — Ray Dalio / Bridgewater
- Decision-making principles encoded as algorithms/rules.
- Back-testing over historical data across countries and time periods.
- Radical transparency + idea meritocracy = stress-test thinking with smartest dissenters.
- Every decision is a bet: favorable when reward × probability > penalty × probability.
- "Know all the possibilities, think about the worst-case scenarios, and then find ways to eliminate the intolerable ones."
- Pain from mistakes converted into system improvement ("pain button").

### 8. Pattern Recognition & Chunking
- Chess grandmasters: recognize positions holistically, not calculate every move.
- Magnus Carlsen blind simultaneous play: 320-piece tracking through compressed pattern libraries.
- Thin-slicing: accurate decisions based on extremely limited data.
- Stress narrows focus; calmness enables pattern access. Cortisol floods system under stress → amygdala dominance → threat perception replaces expansive cognition.
- Elite performers operate in controlled calm, not panic.

### 9. Phronesis (Practical Wisdom)
- Aristotle's term: capability that cannot be taught from manual, lives in pattern-matching against accumulated experience.
- Built case by case, mistake by mistake, consequence by consequence.
- The doctor who departs from protocol because something feels wrong.
- The investor who senses a deal start to break in a way the spreadsheet doesn't show.
- Apprenticeship systems (medicine, Inns of Court, military command, trading floor) as institutional technology for producing phronesis.

### 10. Dominant Narratives Detected
- **Narrative 1 (Klein/NDM):** Expert intuition is real, valuable, and built through experience in high-validity environments. Experts recognize patterns, not options.
- **Narrative 2 (Kahneman):** Human intuition is systematically flawed by heuristics and biases. Statistical models often outperform experts. Confidence is a poor guide to accuracy.
- **Synthesis (Kahneman & Klein 2009):** Both agree on two conditions for skilled intuition: (1) environment of sufficiently high validity, (2) adequate opportunity to practice with feedback.
- **Narrative 3 (Dalio/Ericsson):** Expertise can be accelerated through systematization, deliberate practice, and structured reflection on mistakes.
- **Narrative 4 (Institutional):** Individual bias is hard to overcome; institutional processes (red teams, AARs, peer review) are more reliable debiasing tools.

### 11. Controversies & Conflicting Claims
- Dunning-Kruger "Mount Stupid" curve is a fabrication not in original research; real curve is monotonic.
- ACH (Analysis of Competing Hypotheses) effectiveness is disputed — some studies find mixed evidence, others find it increases errors.
- Deliberate practice framework alone insufficient; individual differences matter but practice behaviors are most controllable.
- Some argue AI/LLMs can replicate or even exceed expert judgment; others argue the 90% tacit knowledge is never documented and lives only in nervous systems of experienced practitioners.

### 12. Gaps Requiring Deeper Investigation
- How exactly do surgeons develop the "feel" that something is wrong before monitors show it?
- What specific institutional mechanisms in emergency medicine produce better vs. worse judgment outcomes?
- How do intelligence agencies operationalize red teams and devil's advocacy in practice?
- What is the neurobiological basis of expert pattern recognition (hippocampus, basal ganglia, prefrontal cortex coordination)?
- How do chess grandmasters' decision processes differ from other experts given the perfect-information environment?
- What is the specific trajectory of confidence change from novice to expert, and what causes the "valley of humility"?
- How do professional investors specifically learn from losses vs. gains?
- Which universal principles are most transferable across domains?
- How can these principles be operationalized into a decision framework (O.M.A.-C.O.R.E.)?
